Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is often misunderstood, with many people viewing it as simply a quirk or a need for cleanliness. However, it's much more complex.

Importantly, people are not "_just a little OCD_".

OCD is a debilitating disorder that far too many go far too long without treatment for due to treatment barriers such as cost and shame associated with the disorder.

### Is There Overlap Between Anxiety and OCD?

In short: YES!

Research consistently highlights the functional similarities between anxiety and OCD.

Both conditions involve heightened responsibility, catastrophic beliefs, and safety-seeking behaviors. These shared features suggest a common underlying mechanism.

In OCD, obsessions are intrusive thoughts causing distress, leading to compulsions aimed at reducing that distress. Anxiety disorders involve excessive fear and safety-seeking behaviors (e.g., avoidance) to reduce that excessive fear.

### The Cognitive Model

Cognitive theories, like those proposed by [Salkovskis (1985)](https://doi.org/10.1016/0005-7967\(85\)90105-6), highlight the intricate thought processes involved. These include thought-action fusion, where thinking about an action is equated with doing it, and the belief that failing to prevent harm is as bad as causing it.

For individuals with OCD, cognitive distortions like thought-action fusion can lead to significant distress and compulsive behaviors aimed at reducing anxiety.

### The Role of Safety-Seeking Behaviors in OCD and Anxiety Disorders

[Safety behaviors](https://jonhlynsson.com/what-are-safety-behaviors/) are actions taken to prevent feared outcomes. While they can initially help individuals engage in anxiety-provoking situations, they may also maintain anxiety by preventing the disconfirmation of anxious thoughts.

In 1999, [Salkovskis and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0005-7967\(98\)00153-3) conducted an experiment investigating the role of safety-seeking behaviors in the maintenance of Panic Disorder with Agoraphobia. Their general conclusion implied that safety-seeking behaviors play an important role in maintaining anxiety because they prevent patients from benefiting from experiences.

Anxiety disorders arise when situations are perceived as more dangerous than they really are and one's ability to cope with that danger is underestimated. Once a threat is misperceived in such a way, at least three mechanisms may be involved in maintaining persistent high levels of anxiety. These mechanisms include _selective attention_ to threat-relevant stimuli, _physiological arousal_, and _safety-seeking behaviors_. (Note that this is **_not_** an exhaustive list).

**The same applies to OCD!**

In OCD, there is selective attention to something that is perceived as threatening. For instance, in the case of fear of contamination, attention becomes more focused on potentially contaminating objects. This results in physiological arousal and safety-seeking behaviors.

Stated differently, one of the safety-seeking behaviors in OCD, upon touching something that is perceived as contaminated, is to avoid touching other things to prevent further contamination.

Safety-seeking behaviors thus play a significant role in OCD, particularly (though far from exclusively) in cases of fear of contamination. For example, washing hands serves as a means to escape the fear of being contaminated, which parallels the mechanisms at play in panic disorder.

### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) offers a structured approach to addressing these issues. It focuses on reducing inflated responsibility beliefs and modifying maladaptive interpretations.

Here’s a simplified step-by-step guide to how CBT can help overcome OCD.

1. **Identify Cognitive Distortions**: Recognize thought-action fusion and other cognitive distortions that contribute to OCD.

3. **Challenge Maladaptive Beliefs**: Use cognitive restructuring to challenge and change these beliefs.

5. **Gradual Exposure**: Systematically expose individuals to feared situations without engaging in safety behaviors.

7. **Response Prevention**: Prevent compulsive responses to reduce reliance on safety behaviors.

9. **Reinforce Learning**: Encourage learning from exposure experiences to disconfirm anxious thoughts.

### Embrace a Functional Perspective

It's crucial for mental health professionals to adopt a functional perspective when treating OCD. This means focusing on the _function_ of behaviors rather than just their form.

By understanding the underlying cognitive processes and addressing them through CBT, we can help individuals break free from the cycle of OCD.
