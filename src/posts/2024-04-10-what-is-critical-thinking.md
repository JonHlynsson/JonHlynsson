As a graduate student, one of the key skills I am to develop is critical thinking. This includes forming informed opinions on research studies, current events, and other daily activities. The goal is to foster some innate ability to critically analyze and synthesize a multitude of information, culminating in some type of wisdom. Be able to juxtapose ideas from various disciplines. Think for my self. Form opinions. But how does one go about fostering such skills? What is the cardinal characteristic of critical thinking?

As far as I'm concerned, critical thinking is made up of different but complementary and interacting components. Below I attempt to make the case that each of the components that typify critical thinking can be collapsed under a higher-order construct I will call curiosity.

### The components of critical thinking

To think critically is to think with intention. Specifically, an intention to learn. For instance, if I have already made up my mind that coffee has a net-positive effect on my life, then I have no reason to delve into the various research articles outlining the detrimental effects coffee in the afternoon can have on my circadian rhythm. Put simply, if I believe coffee is good for me then why should I look at evidence that suggests it negatively impacts my sleep? I wouldn't. I have already decided. I formed my opinion based on what suited my preconceived notion that coffee is good for me.

If I've already made up my mind, then I have no reason to evaluate it my opinion. But therein lies the problem. I have already made up my mind. And worse yet, I don't even want to begin looking at evidence that might run counter to it! After all, doing so might make me question my opinion and induce c_ognitive dissonance_ (the uncomfortable feeling we get when our ideas and actions are unsubstantiated by verifiable facts).

This phenomenon is known as confirmation bias in colloquial terms and describes the tendency to cherry pick information that supports our viewpoint while simotaniously ignoring disconfirming evidence.

Somewhat self-evidently, confirmation bias hinders our ability to learn something new. It also hinders our ability to critically evaluate differing viewpoints, and thus our ability to arrive at our own informed and idiosyncratically derived opionion is impeded. Therefore, thinking with intention to learn is our first cornerstone of critical thinking.

> Thinking with intentionality to learn something new is the first component of critical thinking

Another component of critical thinking revolves around the idea of embracing failure. This may seem paradoxical, but upon closer inspection it is evident that in order to think critically one must make peace with the fact that most of the time your ideas are going to be wrong. This holds for prestigious scholars and laymen alike!

What I mean by this is can be summed up by the following question: What do you learn from being right?

I'd wager that no real learning takes place when we are right. When our current model of the world (including ideas) is supported, the only information we can derive from that is that our idea was correct. Aside from the fact that it probably isn't, there is little room for improvement when things work out exactly as expected. You just get support for your preconceived notion.

However, when we are wrong we have an incalculable opportunity for growth. There is robust evidence in support of the notion that errors are more salient than successes. Being wrong is uncomfortable and this feeling tends to linger much longer than the satisfaction of being right.

What's more is that errors prompt exploration and can thus be harnessed to make great progress towards a valued goal. This links back to a [previous post](https://jonhlynsson.com/what-does-time-mean-to-you/) I made that differentiated incentive rewards from consummatory rewards. Briefly, incentive rewards produce positive emotions when approaching a valued goal, while consummatory rewards produce positive rewards upon goal attainment. Therefore, being wrong and embracing errors can, and should, be levied to promote exploration for why we were wrong in the first place; a venture that can be implicitly rewarding.

The second component of critical thinking can thus be encapsulated in the view that there is no failure, only feedback.

> There is no failure, only feedback

I'm inclined to point out the necessity to engage in your own research. This does not necessarily mean that you must be an academic to think critically. Quite the contrary. Many academics don't think critically as often as they probably should and many non-academics think critically a lot!

What this does mean, however, is that a key component of critical thinking is to scrutinize commonly held beliefs and practices, investigate on your own their validity, and then reach an informed conclusion. In other words, just because something is shared on social media (or even published in an academic journal) does not necessarily make it so.

Evaluating the evidence for your ideas is crucial when it comes to thinking critically. Don't take something anything at face value, especially if there are vested interests that present it to you.

For example, if you come across wet concrete it might be logical to deduce that it had been raining. However, a fire hydrant might have gotten loosed and sprayed water over the pavement, providing the phenomenological illusion of prior rain without there having been any rain at all.

Although this is a simplified example, it cuts into something much deeper: Our proclivity to take things, as they appear to us, at face value. Don't do that. Instead, make a habit of thinking for yourself and evaluating the validity of your ideas and beliefs.

> Think for yourself and have a look at the evidence for yourself

#### A summary of the components of critical thinking

By my estimation, thinking with the intention to learn, embracing failure, and evaluating the appropriate evidence for your viewpoints, all cut at something fundamental. An inquiry based way of being. A curious mindset. Not only does practicing this frame of reference make life more enjoyable (see discussion on rewards above), but it is also humbling.

When David Clark and Adrian Wells we're testing their model for social anxiety, nothing seemed to work to break into the model in a way that derived meaningful differences for social anxiety treatment. They stumbled on the experimental learning module (which became the first component of their social anxiety treatment, a cognitive behavioral therapy model first presented in 1995 and is still considered the gold-standard in treating social anxiety) by curiously persisting through adversity.

What this means is that no matter how accomplished you are, employing a curious mindset that is characterized by a willingness to learn, acceptance of failure, and independent evaluation of the appropriate evidence, will always be advantageous.
