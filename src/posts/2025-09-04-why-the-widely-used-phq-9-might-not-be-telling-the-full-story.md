title: "Why the Widely Used PHQ-9 Might Not Be Telling the Full Story"
date: 2025-09-04
postTags: [research]
---

The Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) is a short, nine-item questionnaire that has become a go-to tool for assessing depression symptoms and even diagnosing major depression in primary care and research.

It's recommended by clinical guidelines and used globally to inform crucial decisions about treatment and clinical trial eligibility.

But what if this widely adopted tool isn't as reliable as we think?

Our recent study titled "_[Why are we still using the PHQ-9? A Historical Review and Psychometric Evaluation of Measurement Invariance](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11126-025-10208-9)_" published in _Psychiatric Quarterly_ raises significant questions about the PHQ-9's validity, particularly its ability to accurately measure depression severity and track changes over time.

**The PHQ-9's Shaky Foundation**

One of the core assumptions of the PHQ-9 is that it measures a single, underlying concept of depression. However, our study, which included 3384 participants from a clinical sample, found that a single-factor structure provided a suboptimal fit for the data.

This means the nine items may not consistently measure just one thing, potentially overlooking the complex, multifaceted nature of depression.

This is especially problematic considering that we as a field have not _really_ been able to define what depression is, and what it isn't (cf. insufficient construct conceptualization and construct contamination).

Using a more rigorous evaluation method called the [Dynamic Fit Index (DFI) framework](https://www.dynamicfit.app/measurement_models/), we concluded that the PHQ-9 model suffered from "severe misspecification".

This is a technical way of saying that the questionnaire **does not** adequately represent a single underlying depressive construct, challenging the practice of simply adding up scores to gauge severity.

### **Inconsistent Measurements Over Time and Across Groups**

Imagine you're tracking your mood during therapy with the PHQ-9. You want to believe that if your score goes down, you're genuinely getting better. Our results suggest that might not always be the case.

We investigated temporal [measurement invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_invariance), which (simply put) one way to evaluate whether a scale measures the same thing consistently over time. Our findings indicated that the PHQ-9 **does not** exhibit strong measurement consistency across repeated administrations during treatment.

This means that **observed changes in PHQ-9 scores might partly reflect shifts in how participants understand or engage with the scale, rather than true changes in their depressive symptoms**.

Furthermore, and even more problematic, we also failed to establish measurement invariance across **different** treatment groups.

In other words, people in different groups (wherein the only differentiating factor between people in group A and group B is random group allocation) may not be interpreting the PHQ-9's items in the same way, making it problematic to compare their scores.

### **Concerns from Conception: A Look at the PHQ-9's Origins**

Adding to these psychometric concerns, we also highlight the questionable origins of the PHQ-9. It was reportedly developed from an idea pitched by a former marketing leader at Pfizer to create a simple screening tool for depression, coinciding with the release of the antidepressant Zoloft. While the developers insisted Pfizer didn't interfere with validation research, the context raises questions about potential conflicts of interest.

## **What Does This Mean for You?**

In essence, our findings raise significant questions about the validity of using the PHQ-9 to track depressive symptom severity and monitor treatment outcomes over time.

Stated differently, simply adding up the item scores may not be a valid indicator of depression status or changes.

In our paper, we argue that it might be time to **"go back to the drawing board"**.

Considering the questionable validity of the most popular instrument to measure depression, coupled with the lack of meaningful advancements in the treatment of depression over the last 25 years, it might be time to invest time and resources to develop a new, theory-driven measure for depression.

Perhaps starting with **qualitative interviews** with patients who have confirmed diagnoses or lived experience to truly understand the breadth and depth of the problem. Then, generating an item set to subject to psychometric evaluation (which would, of course, also align with best practices for scale development!).

Getting the measurement correct (by building bottom-up from theory and qualitative data from people with lived experience). If we achieve that, we could hopefully see better treatment outcomes for depression, which have remained largely unchanged for 25 years.

The message is clear: maintaining the status quo in depression assessment risks stagnating our understanding of mental health and, at worst, potentially harming patients through inaccurate diagnoses and treatments.
